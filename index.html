Three questions from yesterday before we move on. No notes.

Q1 — What’s the difference between IaaS and PaaS? IaaS: you manage the OS, runtime, and application. PaaS: the provider manages everything except your application code.

Q2 — Which cloud model is best for a bank handling customer data? Private cloud. The data can’t legally sit on shared hardware.

Slide 4 — Regions: What Is It?

First question: where in the world are AWS’s servers? The answer is — everywhere. AWS has data centres spread across the globe, and those locations are called Regions.

A Region is a geographic area where AWS has built infrastructure. Think of it as AWS having offices in different cities — each completely independent from the others.

Three things to lock in right now. There are over 30 Regions worldwide and AWS adds new ones every year. Data you put in a Region stays in that Region — it does not automatically replicate to other Regions. And every resource you create in AWS lives in a Region that you choose.

Slide 5 — Regions: Why Do They Exist?

There are three real reasons companies care very deeply about which Region they pick.

First — latency. If your users are in India and your servers are in the US, every request travels across the world. That round trip adds delay, and users feel it. Choosing a Region physically close to your users makes your application faster. A company targeting Indian users picks ap-south-1 in Mumbai, not us-east-1 in Virginia.

Second — compliance. This is often more important than latency. Many countries have laws that say customer data must stay physically within their borders. A German company handling EU customer data may be legally required to keep it in eu-central-1 Frankfurt. This isn’t a preference — it’s a regulatory requirement. Violating it can result in fines.

Third — cost. AWS pricing is not the same in every Region. The exact same server type can cost 10 to 20 percent more in one Region than another. For workloads that have no latency or compliance requirements — like a batch job that processes data overnight — companies sometimes just pick the cheapest Region to reduce their bill.

Slide 6 — Regions: Real Names

These are actual AWS Region codes you will see constantly throughout your career. Take a minute to look at them.

The pattern is always the same — geography, cardinal direction, then a number. us-east-1 means United States, East, first Region in that area. eu-west-1 means Europe, West, first Region. ap-south-1 means Asia Pacific, South, first Region. Once you know the pattern, any Region code is readable.

The most important one to know is us-east-1, Northern Virginia. It’s the original AWS Region. It’s the cheapest. It’s where Amazon itself runs much of its infrastructure, and it’s where most new AWS features launch first before rolling out to other Regions.

For us here in India — ap-south-1 Mumbai is your Region. Lowest latency for Indian users and the most relevant for anything you build for an Indian audience.

Slide 7 — Regions: AWS Global Infrastructure

Now let’s zoom out and look at the full picture. AWS’s global infrastructure isn’t just Regions — it has three distinct layers, each serving a different purpose.

The first layer is Regions. 30+ worldwide. Full data centre clusters — compute, storage, networking, databases, everything. This is where you deploy your applications.

The second layer is Availability Zones. 100+ globally. Multiple isolated data centres sitting inside each Region. The resilience layer. We’ll cover these in depth in a moment.

The third layer is Edge Locations. 400+ worldwide. These are much smaller than Regions — not full data centres, just lightweight cache points. They exist in cities and towns that don’t have a full AWS Region. Their job is to serve content to end users as fast as possible, without the request having to travel to a full Region.

The number to remember: 30+ Regions, 100+ AZs, 400+ Edge Locations. Each layer gets larger in count but smaller in capability as you go down.

Slide 8 — Regions: Edge Locations & CloudFront

Let me explain Edge Locations with a concrete example you’ll recognise. Have you ever noticed that Netflix streams smoothly even though Netflix’s servers aren’t in your city?

That’s because of a CDN — a Content Delivery Network. AWS’s CDN is called CloudFront, and it uses those 400+ Edge Locations to make it work.

Here’s the flow. Step 1 — Netflix uploads a video file to S3, which is sitting in us-east-1, Virginia. Step 2 — they enable CloudFront. CloudFront automatically copies that video file to hundreds of Edge Locations worldwide, including Mumbai. Step 3 — a user in Mumbai hits play. Instead of that request going to Virginia and back, CloudFront serves the video from the Mumbai Edge Location — milliseconds away. Step 4 — the video loads instantly. The user never touches the Virginia Region at all.

This is the difference between a Region and an Edge Location. Regions are where you build and run your infrastructure. Edge Locations are where you bring content close to users.

Slide 9 — AZs: What Is It?

Now let’s zoom inside a Region. A Region is not just one data centre — it contains multiple, and they are called Availability Zones or AZs.

An Availability Zone is one or more physical data centres within a Region, with completely independent power, cooling, and networking. The word independent is doing a lot of work there.

Look at the diagram on screen. us-east-1, Northern Virginia. Inside it, three AZs — us-east-1a, us-east-1b, us-east-1c. They’re all in the same general geographic area — a few kilometres apart. But a power cut in us-east-1a does not affect us-east-1b. A network failure in us-east-1c does not affect us-east-1a. They are physically isolated from each other by design.

Most Regions have 3 Availability Zones. They’re close enough that network traffic between them is fast — typically under 2 milliseconds. But far enough apart that a single physical event — fire, flood, power failure — cannot take down more than one.

Slide 10 — AZs vs Regions

This is the distinction that trips students up most often. Let me be very explicit.

A Region is the geographic container. us-east-1 is a Region. Think of it like a city.

An Availability Zone is inside that Region — a specific cluster of data centres. us-east-1a is an AZ inside us-east-1. Think of it like a building in that city.

The distance between Regions is thousands of kilometres — us-east-1 to eu-west-1 is across the Atlantic Ocean. The distance between AZs is a few kilometres within the same metropolitan area.

Regions serve different purposes than AZs. You choose a Region for geographic, compliance, or cost reasons. You use multiple AZs within that Region for resilience — to protect your application from a single data centre failure.

A full Region going down is extremely rare — it has happened a handful of times in AWS history and is treated as a major crisis. A single AZ having a problem is much more plausible, and happens more regularly. That’s exactly why AZs exist.

Slide 11 — AZs: Why AZs Matter

This is the most important thing I will say today. I want you to genuinely understand this, not just memorise it.

If you deploy your application in one AZ only, and that data centre has a power failure, your application is gone. Completely down. Every user gets an error. That is called a single point of failure, and it is unacceptable for any production application.

Look at the two diagrams. On the left — single AZ deployment. Your app lives in us-east-1a. us-east-1a loses power. Your app is down. Nothing else is running.

On the right — multi-AZ deployment. Your app runs simultaneously in us-east-1a, us-east-1b, and us-east-1c. us-east-1a loses power. Traffic automatically shifts to us-east-1b and us-east-1c. Users experience nothing — the app keeps running. Nobody gets paged at 3am.

The rule to remember for your entire career: any production application should run across at least two Availability Zones. This is not optional. This is not advanced. This is the baseline. AWS makes it easy — most services have a single checkbox or dropdown to enable multi-AZ deployment. There is no excuse for skipping it.

Slide 12 — AZs: Real AWS Outage Case Study

Let me make this real. In December 2021, AWS had a major outage in us-east-1 that affected hundreds of services and lasted most of the day.

At around 10:30am, AWS’s own networking automation triggered an unexpected configuration change. Internal AWS services started failing.

By 11am it had cascaded — AWS’s own monitoring tools went down. This is significant because it meant the engineers trying to fix the problem couldn’t easily see what was broken.

By noon, services that depended on us-east-1 — Disney+, Netflix, Slack, Coinbase, Amazon.com itself — all started having problems. These are companies running billion-dollar infrastructure on AWS.

By 3pm, AWS engineers had isolated and fixed the root cause. But full recovery took several more hours after that.

The lesson: even AWS can fail. Companies that had multi-Region architectures — traffic running in eu-west-1 or ap-southeast-1 as well — recovered much faster. Companies that had everything in us-east-1 with no fallback were stuck waiting.

This is not a theoretical risk. This happened.

Slide 13 — AZs: How to Pick an AZ

A common question: which AZ should I use? The answer is a bit more nuanced than you might expect.

AZ names like us-east-1a, us-east-1b, us-east-1c are logical labels — not fixed physical locations. Crucially, the same label maps to different physical data centres for different AWS accounts. If your us-east-1a and my us-east-1a are actually different physical buildings, that’s intentional.

Why? Because if everyone assumed us-east-1a was the “first” and “best” AZ and put all their resources there, it would become overloaded. AWS randomises the physical mapping per account to spread load evenly across all the data centres.

The practical rule is simple: don’t try to optimise for a specific AZ. Just make sure your resources are spread across at least two or three different AZs in the same Region. Don’t put everything in one. AWS handles the physical distribution.

One more thing worth knowing: AZs within a Region talk to each other at very low latency — typically under 2 milliseconds. That’s fast enough to run databases in synchronous replication across AZs, which is exactly how RDS Multi-AZ works. And some services like RDS and Auto Scaling groups can automatically spread across AZs for you — you just specify the Region and they handle the rest.

Slide 14 — Console: What Is It?

Now let’s talk about the tool you’ll actually use every day. The AWS Console is the web interface for accessing and managing all AWS services. You access it at console.aws.amazon.com. It’s just a website — no installation, no setup, just a browser.

Three things to understand before you ever open it.

First — all 200+ AWS services are accessible from one place. EC2, S3, RDS, IAM, Lambda, CloudWatch — everything lives in this single console. It can feel overwhelming at first. That’s normal.

Second — the Region selector is critical. Everything you see in the console is scoped to whichever Region you currently have selected. Switch Region and you’re looking at a completely different set of resources.

Third — the search bar is your fastest way to navigate. Don’t memorise the menus. Type the service name and press Enter. That’s how experienced engineers navigate it too.

Slide 15 — Console: Key Areas

Six areas you need to know before you open the console.

One — the search bar at the top. Type any service name, press Enter. Fastest way to get anywhere.

Two — the Region selector, top right. The most important element on the entire page. Check it before you do anything. Every resource you create goes into the Region shown here.

Three — the Account menu, also top right. Your account name, billing information, settings. You’ll use this for checking costs and managing access.

Four — the Services menu, top left. Lets you browse all services by category — Compute, Storage, Database, Networking, Machine Learning. Useful when you’re exploring, but slower than just searching.

Five — Recently visited. After a few days of working in AWS, this panel becomes your shortcut list. The services you use most will appear here automatically.

Six — Console Home dashboard. Shows recently used services and any health alerts for your account. Worth checking when you first log in.

Slide 16 — Console: The #1 Mistake

Here is the most common confusion beginners experience, so you don’t have to go through it yourself.

You open the EC2 dashboard — we’ll cover EC2 properly next class. You look around, then switch Region to explore a different one. Then you switch back. The page looks completely different — things you saw before are gone. You start to panic.

Nothing broke. Nothing was deleted. You are simply looking at a different Region. AWS is showing you the resources that exist in the Region you currently have selected, and that Region is empty because you haven’t created anything there. Switch back to the original Region and everything is exactly where you left it.

The habit to build, starting today: every single time you open the AWS console, before you click on anything, look at the top right corner, read the Region name, confirm it is where you intend to work, and then proceed. Do this every time. Make it automatic.

Slide 17 — Console: IAM Deep Dive

IAM — Identity and Access Management — is the most important security service in all of AWS. It is global, meaning it applies to your entire account regardless of Region. And it is completely free.

IAM has four main concepts you need to understand.

Users are individual people or applications that need access to AWS. Each person should have their own IAM user with their own credentials. Never share credentials between people — if something goes wrong you won’t know who did it.

Groups are collections of users. Instead of assigning permissions to each user individually, you assign a policy to a group and all users in that group inherit it. You might have a Developers group, an Admins group, a ReadOnly group.

Roles are like users but for AWS services or external systems. Imagine an EC2 instance that needs to read files from S3. Instead of hardcoding credentials into the application — which is dangerous — you assign the EC2 instance a Role that grants it S3 read permission. No credentials stored anywhere.

Policies are JSON documents that define what is allowed or denied. You attach policies to users, groups, or roles. The most powerful policy is AdministratorAccess — it grants full access to everything in the account.

We will go much deeper on IAM later in the course. For now, just understand that it exists and that these four concepts are the foundation of AWS security.

Slide 18 — Console: Billing & Cost Management

One of the most important skills in DevOps is understanding what you’re paying for and why. AWS charges for almost everything — compute time, storage, data transfer, API calls. Unexpected bills are a real problem, even for experienced engineers.

Cost Explorer is a visual tool inside the console that shows your spending over time, broken down by service, Region, or resource tag. If a bill looks higher than expected, Cost Explorer is the first place you go to investigate.

Billing Alerts let you set a budget threshold — say $10 per month — and AWS will email you when you’re approaching or exceeding it. This is essential for any personal or student account. Set one before you start creating resources.

The AWS Free Tier gives new accounts 12 months of limited free usage. This includes 750 hours per month of a t2.micro EC2 instance, 5 GB of S3 storage, and more. Always check whether a service is free-tier eligible before using it.

And finally — common bill shocks. These happen when someone forgets to stop an EC2 instance after class, leaves a database running overnight, or accidentally enables a premium service without realising it. The golden rule: if you created it, turn it off when you’re done with it.

Slide 19 — Console: CloudWatch

CloudWatch is AWS’s built-in monitoring and observability service. It’s the tool that answers the question: “What is actually happening in my infrastructure right now?”

Every AWS service sends data to CloudWatch automatically. You don’t have to configure anything to start collecting basic metrics.

Metrics are numerical measurements over time — CPU usage, memory, network traffic, request counts, error rates. CloudWatch collects these from every service and makes them queryable and graphable.

Logs are the text output from your applications and AWS services. CloudWatch Logs stores them, lets you search them, and lets you filter for specific patterns — like error messages.

Alarms let you set thresholds on any metric. If CPU usage goes above 80% for five minutes, CloudWatch can send you an email, trigger an automatic scaling action, or run a Lambda function. Alarms are how you get notified before things break rather than after.

Dashboards let you build visual displays combining metrics from multiple services. Operations teams watch these during incidents to understand the state of a system at a glance.

CloudWatch is one of the first tools you check when something goes wrong. We’ll use it hands-on in a later class.

Slide 20 — Console: Key Services to Know

Before we close the Console section, here’s a quick map of the six core services you’ll encounter throughout this course.

EC2 — Elastic Compute Cloud. Virtual machines in the cloud. You rent a server, control it completely, and pay by the hour. We cover this next class.

S3 — Simple Storage Service. Object storage for any kind of file — images, videos, backups, logs, static websites. Globally unique bucket names. Practically unlimited storage.

RDS — Relational Database Service. Managed MySQL, PostgreSQL, Oracle, and others. AWS handles backups, patching, and failover. You just use the database.

IAM — we just covered this. Users, Groups, Roles, Policies. The security foundation for everything in AWS.

VPC — Virtual Private Cloud. Your own private network inside AWS. Controls what can talk to what, what’s public-facing and what’s internal. Every EC2 instance lives inside a VPC.

Lambda — serverless compute. You write a function, upload it to Lambda, and it runs when triggered. No server to manage. Pay only for the milliseconds it actually runs.

Slide 21 — How It All Fits Together

Now let me connect everything we’ve covered today into one coherent picture.

AWS services operate at three different scopes, and this matters enormously in practice.

Global services like IAM, Route 53, and CloudFront operate across all Regions simultaneously. When you create an IAM user, that user exists everywhere in your account. There’s no Region selector for these services — one change affects everything.

Regional services like S3, DynamoDB, and Lambda exist per Region. An S3 bucket you create in us-east-1 is a completely separate bucket from one you create in ap-south-1. When you create these resources, you choose the Region, and that’s where they live.

AZ-level services like EC2, RDS, and EBS volumes live inside a specific Availability Zone. When you launch an EC2 instance, it ends up in a specific AZ — say us-east-1a. If you want resilience, you need to manually deploy instances across multiple AZs, or use a service that does it for you.

Understanding which scope a service operates at is one of the most useful mental models you’ll build in this course. When something isn’t visible where you expect it — check the Region. When you want resilience — check the AZ.

Slide 22 — Quick Quiz

Five questions. Think before looking at the answers.

Q1 — A company in Germany must store EU customer data within Europe. Which AWS concept drives that decision? Region. They need eu-central-1 Frankfurt to comply with data residency law.

Q2 — Your app runs in us-east-1a only. The data centre loses power. What happens? The app goes down completely. Single AZ, single point of failure. This is exactly what multi-AZ is designed to prevent.

Q3 — You open the AWS console and type a service name in the top bar to navigate. What are you using? The console search bar.

Q4 — An Indian startup wants the lowest latency for users in Mumbai. Which Region should they choose? ap-south-1.

Q5 — A Netflix video loads quickly in Mumbai even though Netflix’s servers are in the US. Which AWS concept makes this possible? Edge Locations and CloudFront — the video is cached at the Mumbai edge location.

Slide 23 — Key Takeaways

Three things to leave today with.

Regions — a physical location in the world where AWS runs full infrastructure. You choose based on latency, compliance, or cost. ap-south-1 is Mumbai and closest to us. us-east-1 is Virginia and the biggest and cheapest. 30+ worldwide.

Availability Zones — isolated data centres inside a Region, each with independent power and networking. Always deploy across at least two AZs in production. Single AZ is a single point of failure. The December 2021 outage showed that even AWS itself isn’t immune to failures.

Console — console.aws.amazon.com. Check the Region selector before doing anything. Search bar is your fastest navigation tool. IAM, Billing, and CloudWatch are three of the most important services to understand before you start building.
