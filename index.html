W1D3 Teaching Script — Public vs Private vs Hybrid Cloud

Slide 1 — Title

Good morning everyone. Day 3. Yesterday we covered IaaS, PaaS, and SaaS — the different ways you consume cloud services. Today we’re asking a completely different question. Not what you get from the cloud, but where that cloud actually lives. Who owns the hardware? Who else is on it? Can another company be running their app on the same physical machine as you?

That’s what Public, Private, and Hybrid Cloud are about. Let’s get into it.

Slide 2 — Agenda

Same structure as yesterday. Quick recap, then we go one model at a time. Each model gets its own block — definition, pros and cons, examples, and a real use case. Then we compare all three, I’ll give you a decision guide, quick quiz, and 10 minutes for questions.

Slide 3 — The Core Question

Before I explain anything, I want to re-anchor us. Yesterday the question was: how much do you manage yourself? Today’s question is different — where does the infrastructure physically live?

Read through these three. Public cloud means the infrastructure is owned by a provider like AWS, and shared between thousands of different customers. Private cloud means it’s dedicated entirely to your organisation — nobody else touches it. Hybrid is a mix of both, connected together.

Keep those three definitions in your head. Everything today builds on them.

Slide 4 — Public Cloud: What Is It?

Public cloud is what most people picture when they say “the cloud.” The servers, storage, and networking are owned and operated by a third-party provider. And importantly — it’s shared. When you run a VM on AWS, somewhere in an Amazon data centre, your virtual machine is on the same physical hardware as some other company’s virtual machine. AWS handles the isolation between you, but you’re sharing the box.

You pay for what you use, exactly like an electricity bill. Three things to remember: pay as you go, scale instantly, available from anywhere in the world.

Slide 5 — Public Cloud: Pros & Cons

Let’s be honest about what’s good and what’s not.

The pros: no hardware to buy upfront, scales up or down instantly on demand, zero maintenance because the provider handles everything, and it’s available globally across every region.

The cons: your data shares physical hardware with other companies — that’s the shared tenancy model. You have less control over the underlying infrastructure. It requires internet connectivity to work. And costs can spike if you’re not paying attention to what you’re running.

Being able to name both sides is what makes you credible in a technical conversation.

Slide 6 — Public Cloud: Real Examples

These are the names you’ll hear constantly. AWS — Amazon Web Services — holds about a third of the entire cloud market. Netflix, Airbnb, NASA all run on AWS.

Microsoft Azure is number two, dominant in large enterprises that are already deep in the Microsoft ecosystem.

Google Cloud Platform is third, and it’s especially strong in data analytics and machine learning.

DigitalOcean is much smaller but beloved by developers because it’s simpler and cheaper. You’ll likely use it yourselves for personal projects.

Slide 7 — Public Cloud: Use Case

Real scenario. A two-person startup is building a food delivery app. Zero budget for servers.

They sign up for AWS that afternoon, spin up a few EC2 instances, and the app is live worldwide. They’re paying about $50 a month.

Six months later — 100,000 users. Weekend traffic is ten times higher than weekday traffic. AWS auto-scales the servers automatically. They changed nothing in their setup.

Key insight: zero upfront cost, pay only for what they use, and scale without touching anything. This is why almost every startup in the world defaults to public cloud. The financial barrier to starting is basically gone.

Slide 8 — Private Cloud: What Is It?

Private cloud — the name says it all. The infrastructure is dedicated entirely to one organisation. No other company shares your hardware, period.

It can be physically in your own data centre, or a provider can give you dedicated hardware at their facility — but either way, no one else is on it.

Three things to remember: maximum security through complete isolation, full control over every layer of the stack, and it satisfies the strictest compliance requirements — which is the main reason companies actually choose it.

Slide 9 — Private Cloud: Pros & Cons

Pros: complete data isolation — no shared hardware whatsoever. Full control over every layer. It meets strict regulatory and compliance requirements that public cloud simply can’t satisfy. And you can customise everything to your exact specifications.

Cons: very high upfront cost — you’re buying hardware, building facilities, and hiring staff. It’s slow to scale because you’re limited by what physical hardware you actually own. You are personally responsible for all maintenance and upgrades. And you need a dedicated in-house infrastructure team just to keep it running.

This is why most companies never go private. The economics only make sense when the legal or security requirement forces your hand.

Slide 10 — Private Cloud: Who Uses It?

If private cloud is this expensive and complex, why does it exist? Almost always because organisations are legally required to use it, or because the risk of shared hardware is genuinely unacceptable.

Banks — customer financial data often legally cannot sit on shared infrastructure. Regulations in many countries require isolation.

Healthcare — patient medical records require strict data isolation. Laws like HIPAA in the US set very specific requirements.

Government — national security data cannot run on AWS where thousands of other companies also have workloads.

Large enterprises with existing data centres — if a company has spent hundreds of millions on physical infrastructure over decades, they don’t abandon it overnight. They build a private cloud on top.

Research institutions — universities and labs with sensitive research data or proprietary experiments they can’t risk on shared infrastructure.

Slide 11 — Private Cloud: Use Case

Here’s the scenario. A major bank processes millions of transactions every day — account data, loan records, payment history.

The regulator says: this data must stay within the country and must never sit on shared hardware. So the bank builds their own private cloud inside their own data centre. They virtualise their physical servers, add automation on top, and operate it like a cloud — but it’s entirely theirs.

Key insight: no other company can ever touch their data. Zero shared hardware. But the trade-off is real — it costs enormously more to build and operate, and they need a dedicated team of engineers just to keep the lights on. That’s the price of full isolation.

Slide 12 — Hybrid Cloud: What Is It?

Hybrid cloud is a combination of public and private cloud working together as one environment.

Look at the diagram. On the left — private cloud. Locked down, dedicated, for sensitive data and compliance workloads. On the right — public cloud. Flexible, cheap, for everything else. In the middle, they’re connected. Data and applications can flow securely between them.

The idea is you don’t have to choose one or the other. Keep the sensitive stuff private. Put everything else on public. Get the security benefits of private and the cost and scale benefits of public — in the same architecture.

Slide 13 — Hybrid Cloud: Pros & Cons

Pros: sensitive data stays isolated on private infrastructure where it legally needs to be. Public cloud handles scale and cost for everything else. It enables gradual migration from legacy systems instead of a risky big-bang cutover. And you can set up disaster recovery across both environments.

Cons: this is the most complex architecture of the three — it’s harder to design and manage. You need expertise in both public and private cloud. The secure connection between the two environments adds another layer of complexity. And it can be expensive if you get the balance wrong between what runs where.

Hybrid is powerful, but it’s not something you build on day one. It’s where mature organisations end up after years of running both environments.

Slide 14 — Hybrid Cloud: Why Companies Use It

Four real reasons you’ll see in the industry.

First — they already have a data centre. You can’t migrate everything to public cloud in a year. Hybrid lets them use what they’ve already paid for while slowly moving workloads over time.

Second — some data legally must stay private. A hospital’s patient records have to stay on private infrastructure — but their patient-facing website can run on AWS without any compliance issues.

Third — cost optimisation. Running steady baseline workloads on private infrastructure can actually be cheaper long-term than paying public cloud rates. But when traffic spikes, you burst to the public cloud rather than buying hardware that would sit idle most of the year.

Fourth — disaster recovery. Primary systems on private. Backup and failover on public cloud. If the data centre has a problem, the public cloud takes over automatically.

Slide 15 — Hybrid Cloud: Use Case

Here’s the scenario. A hospital chain has patient records, diagnoses, and medical history for millions of patients. This data must legally stay on private servers within the country. Non-negotiable.

But they also run a patient appointment booking website. During flu season, thousands of people use it at the same time. Running that on private cloud would mean buying expensive hardware that sits mostly idle for the other ten months of the year.

So the architecture splits: patient data on private cloud — protected, compliant, isolated. The booking website on AWS — auto-scaling with demand, cost-efficient.

Both systems talk to each other over a secure private connection. That’s hybrid cloud in the real world — each workload in the right environment.

Slide 16 — Side-by-Side Comparison

Let’s put all three next to each other. Take a minute to read through the table.

The pattern is clear. As you go from public to private, cost goes up, security goes up, but scalability and ease both go down. Hybrid sits in the middle of almost every dimension.

The examples row: Netflix on AWS for public, a bank’s internal systems for private, a hospital’s split architecture for hybrid. Those three examples will serve you well in interviews and conversations.

Slide 17 — When Do You Choose Which?

This is the practical decision guide. When a client or employer asks “what cloud model should we use?” — this is how you think through it.

Public cloud if: you’re a startup or small company, your data has no strict legal requirements, you need low cost and fast scale.

Private cloud if: you’re in banking, healthcare, or government, regulations force data isolation, you have existing infrastructure to leverage, you need full control.

Hybrid if: you have legacy systems that can’t be fully migrated yet, some workloads need private but others need public scale, you want disaster recovery across both, you need to optimise cost versus security on a per-workload basis.

Slide 18 — Quick Quiz

Four scenarios. Public, Private, or Hybrid — don’t overthink it.

Q1 — A startup launches on AWS. No data centre, pay monthly. — Public. No reason for anything more complex.

Q2 — A bank builds their own virtualised infrastructure in-house for customer data. — Private. Legal requirement, dedicated hardware.

Q3 — A hospital keeps patient records on-premises but runs their website on Google Cloud. — Hybrid. Two environments, one connected architecture.

Q4 — Netflix stores and streams all their video content via AWS worldwide. — Public. Massive scale, global infrastructure, no regulatory reason to go private.

Slide 19 — Key Takeaways

Three one-liners.

Public Cloud — provider owns everything, pay as you go, best for most companies especially startups. Think: shared office space. You rent a desk, other companies are in the same building.

Private Cloud — you own everything, most secure, most expensive, required for regulated industries. Think: your own building that only your company uses.

Hybrid Cloud — mix of both, sensitive workloads stay private, everything else goes public, most large enterprises end up here eventually. Think: your own secure vault for the important stuff, rented space for everything else.
