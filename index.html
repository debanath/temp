W1D2 Teaching Script — IaaS vs PaaS vs SaaS

Slide 1 — Title

Alright everyone, good morning. Today is Day 2 — yesterday we talked about what cloud computing is and why it exists. Today we’re going one level deeper. We’re going to talk about the three ways you can actually consume cloud services — IaaS, PaaS, and SaaS. By the end of this class, you’ll be able to look at any cloud product and immediately tell me which category it falls into. Let’s get into it.

Slide 2 — Agenda

Here’s how we’re spending the next hour. We’ll start with a quick two-minute recap of yesterday, then we’ll go through each model one by one — IaaS, PaaS, SaaS. Each one gets about 15 minutes. Then we’ll do a comparison table, a short quiz, and I’ll leave the last 10 minutes for your questions. Simple structure, let’s stick to it.

Slide 3 — The Core Question

Before I explain anything technical, I want you to think about this. When you rent a car, you drive it yourself — you’re in control, but you’re also responsible for everything. When you take a taxi, someone else drives — you give up some control but you don’t have to worry about the road. When you use Uber, you just tap a button — maximum convenience, zero responsibility for how it works under the hood.

Cloud services work exactly the same way. The only real difference between IaaS, PaaS, and SaaS is how much you manage yourself versus how much the provider handles for you. More control on one end, more convenience on the other. Keep that in your head — everything else today is just details on top of this idea.

Slide 4 — The Pizza Analogy

Now let me make this even more concrete with the pizza analogy — this is the most famous way people explain cloud models, and once you hear it, you won’t forget it.

If you make pizza at home — that’s like on-premises. You buy every ingredient, you make the dough, you cook it, you serve it. You own everything and you do everything.

If you do take and bake — that’s IaaS. Someone gives you the ingredients and the oven, but you still prepare it and cook it yourself.

If you order pizza delivery — that’s PaaS. The pizza arrives ready. You might add some toppings, but someone else did all the hard work.

If you go to a restaurant — that’s SaaS. You sit down, you order, the chef cooks, the waiter serves. You just eat. Nothing to manage.

The cloud equivalents are right there at the bottom — AWS EC2 for IaaS, Heroku for PaaS, Gmail for SaaS. We’ll go deeper into each one now.

Slide 5 — IaaS: What Is It?

Okay, IaaS — Infrastructure as a Service. The core idea is simple: the cloud provider gives you raw servers, storage, and networking. That’s it. Just the hardware, virtualised.

You rent a virtual machine and then you build everything on top of it — the operating system, the runtime, the database, your application. Everything above the hardware is your job.

Three things to remember about IaaS: you have the most control out of all three models, but you also have the most responsibility, and it’s best suited for teams that need a very custom or specific setup. If you want to decide exactly which version of Ubuntu runs, exactly how MySQL is configured, exactly how your backups work — IaaS is your answer.

Slide 6 — IaaS: What You Manage

Look at this diagram. On the left side — coloured in green, yellow, orange, red — those are all the things YOU are responsible for with IaaS. Your application, your data, the runtime like Node or Python, the operating system, and virtualisation.

On the right side — in grey — are the things the provider manages. Physical servers, storage, networking. They handle the actual hardware so you don’t have to buy it or maintain it.

Think of it like leasing office space. The landlord gives you the building. But you buy the furniture, set up the desks, run the cables, install everything. The building is theirs — everything inside is yours to set up and maintain.

Slide 7 — IaaS: Real Examples

These are the actual products you’ll hear about in the industry. AWS EC2 is the most popular — EC2 stands for Elastic Compute Cloud, and it’s essentially Amazon’s virtual machine service. Azure Virtual Machines is Microsoft’s version, and it’s heavily used in enterprise companies. Google Compute Engine is Google’s offering. And DigitalOcean Droplets is very popular with individual developers and small startups because it’s simpler and cheaper.

When someone in a job interview asks you about IaaS, these are the names you drop. EC2 especially — you will hear that name constantly in this course.

Slide 8 — IaaS: Real Use Case

Let me make this real with a scenario. A company called FinanceApp needs to run a custom database server. They have very specific requirements — a particular version of MySQL, a specific configuration that a managed service doesn’t support.

So here’s what they do: they rent an EC2 instance on AWS, they SSH into it, they install Ubuntu, they install MySQL themselves, they configure it, and they set up their own backup scripts.

See that row of steps at the bottom? Every single one of those is the company’s job. They did it all.

Here’s the key insight — with IaaS, they have total control. But if MySQL crashes at 2am, it’s their engineer who gets woken up. That’s the trade-off. Control comes with responsibility.

Slide 9 — PaaS: What Is It?

Now PaaS — Platform as a Service. This is where it starts getting more convenient.

With PaaS, the provider doesn’t just give you servers. They give you the entire environment your app runs in. That means the runtime is already there, the OS is already configured, scaling is handled automatically. You just write your code and deploy it.

Think about what that means for a developer. They don’t have to think about “which version of Node is installed” or “how do I configure this server” or “how do I make it scale when traffic increases.” All of that is the platform’s job. The developer just focuses on the application.

Three things to remember about PaaS: just write code and deploy, it’s much faster to launch than IaaS, and it’s best for developers and startups who want to move quickly.

Slide 10 — PaaS: What You Manage

Same diagram as before but notice what changed. On the left — only your application and your data. That’s all you manage. Just two layers.

Everything else — runtime, OS, virtualisation, physical servers — all handled by the provider. That’s a lot less work compared to IaaS.

The analogy here is a fully equipped kitchen for rent. The stove, the oven, the counters — all set up and ready. You walk in, cook your dish, and walk out. You didn’t have to install anything. You just used it.

Slide 11 — PaaS: Real Examples

Heroku is the most beginner-friendly PaaS — you’ll probably use it yourself in this course. You connect your GitHub repo, push code, and it’s live. That’s genuinely it.

Google App Engine automatically scales your app based on traffic — if a million people visit your site at once, it handles it without you doing anything.

AWS Elastic Beanstalk is Amazon’s PaaS — it’s built on top of EC2 but abstracts all the complexity away.

Render and Railway are newer options that are very popular with developers today — they’re basically modern Heroku.

Slide 12 — PaaS: Real Use Case

Let me give you a real scenario. Aisha is a developer at a startup. She builds a Node.js web app. Instead of renting a server, installing Node, configuring Nginx, setting up SSL — she just runs one command: git push heroku main.

Heroku detects it’s a Node.js app automatically. It installs the dependencies, starts the server, sets up HTTPS, and gives her a live URL. The whole thing takes maybe 30 seconds.

The key insight here is what Aisha didn’t do. She didn’t touch a single server. She didn’t install anything. She didn’t configure anything. She just wrote the app and pushed it.

That’s the power of PaaS — and that’s why startups love it.

Slide 13 — SaaS: What Is It?

And now SaaS — Software as a Service. This is the simplest model of all three.

The provider handles absolutely everything. Infrastructure, platform, and the full application. You just open a browser, log in, and use it. There’s nothing to install on a server, nothing to configure, no updates to run manually. It just works.

Here’s something interesting — most people in the world are already heavy SaaS users without knowing what the term means. Your parents use SaaS. Your non-technical friends use SaaS. If you’ve ever used Gmail, you’ve used SaaS.

Three things to remember: zero setup, always up to date automatically, and best for end users and businesses who just need a tool that works.

Slide 14 — SaaS: Apps You Already Use

I want you to look at this slide and tell me honestly — how many of these do you use every day?

Gmail or Outlook — you never set up a mail server. WhatsApp or Slack — you just installed the app and started chatting. Zoom — you open a browser and join a call. Google Drive — your files are in the cloud, no hardware needed. Spotify or Netflix — you just press play. Notion or Figma — tools that dev teams and designers use daily.

Every single one of these is SaaS. The companies behind them manage massive server infrastructure, databases, security, updates — and you see none of it. You just get the product.

Slide 15 — SaaS: What You Manage

Look at this diagram. On the left — only your data. What you type, what you upload. That is the only thing you’re responsible for.

Everything else — the app itself, the runtime, the OS, the database, the servers, the networking — all handled by the provider. Everything.

The restaurant analogy is perfect here. You sit down, you order, the chef cooks everything, the waiter brings it. You pay and enjoy. You’re not responsible for the kitchen, the ingredients, the cleaning, the electricity bill of the restaurant. None of it. You just show up and eat.

Slide 16 — SaaS: Real Use Case

Here’s the scenario. A 50-person company decides to use Slack for communication. What do they do? They go to slack.com, sign up, enter their card details, and send invite links to the team. That’s the entire setup process.

No servers to provision. No app to deploy. No database to manage. No updates to schedule. Slack’s engineers handle all of that. The company just pays a monthly fee and uses the product.

And here’s the key insight — the company manages zero infrastructure. The only thing they manage is their own data — their messages and files. If Slack’s servers go down, it’s Slack’s problem to fix. Not theirs.

Slide 17 — Side-by-Side Comparison

Okay let’s put all three side by side now. Take a minute to read through this table.

See the pattern? As you go from IaaS to PaaS to SaaS, the amount you manage keeps shrinking. With IaaS you manage the OS, runtime, app, and data. With PaaS just the app and data. With SaaS just your data.

At the same time, control goes from high to medium to low, and ease of use goes from complex to moderate to very easy. These two things always move together — the more control you have, the more complex it is.

The examples column is the one I want you to remember: EC2, Heroku, Gmail. Those three names map perfectly to IaaS, PaaS, SaaS.

Slide 18 — Quick Quiz

Alright, close your notes. Let’s see if this landed. I’ll read each scenario — you just say IaaS, PaaS, or SaaS. Don’t overthink it.

Q1 — A startup rents a virtual machine on AWS and installs their own web server and database. What is that? — IaaS. They’re managing everything on top of the raw machine.

Q2 — A developer pushes code to Heroku with one command and the platform runs it automatically. — PaaS. The platform handled everything except the app.

Q3 — Your team uses Google Workspace for email and docs. No setup required. — SaaS. You just log in and use it.

Q4 — Netflix runs thousands of virtual machines on AWS to stream video. — IaaS. Netflix manages those VMs themselves — they just rent the raw compute from Amazon.

Good. If you got all four, you’ve understood today’s class.

Slide 19 — Key Takeaways

Let me leave you with the one-liner for each.

IaaS — raw infrastructure, you manage everything above the hardware, think renting a car.

PaaS — platform provided, you just manage your app and data, think calling a taxi.

SaaS — full product delivered, you manage nothing technical, think Uber — tap and go.
